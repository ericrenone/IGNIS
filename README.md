## The Formal Claim

Cooking is the oldest and most consequential information technology in human evolutionary history. Not a tool — an information technology. Cooking transforms the chemical information structure of food: it denatures proteins (making amino acid sequences accessible to digestive enzymes that could not reach them in the folded state), gelatinizes starches (converting resistant polysaccharide structures into rapidly fermentable substrates), breaks down antinutrients (phytates, oxalates, lectins), and generates new flavor compounds (the Maillard reaction, caramelization) that signal nutrient density and pathogen-free preparation to the olfactory and gustatory systems.

The formal claim of IGNIS: cooking is the externalization of digestion — the transfer of metabolic work from the gut (the body's internal chemical Commons) to fire and culture (the external Commons). This transfer is not incidental to human evolution. It is the mechanism through which the FERN register crossing from *Australopithecus*-level cognition to *Homo erectus*-level cognition was made energetically possible. And the modern ultra-processing crisis is the same mechanism in reverse: external food processing has been taken so far that it has destroyed the gut microbiome Commons — the internal $X_{t-1}$ that all subsequent metabolic contributions condition on.

IGNIS applies the ERI framework to the complete history of human cooking: six register crossings, each corresponding to a structural transformation in how the cooking Commons accumulates, and a current $G_{\text{coord}} < 0$ crisis that mirrors the respiratory Commons crisis documented in PNEUMA.

## Part I — Before Fire: The Pre-Cooking Externalization Chain (7–2 Mya)

### Four Forms of External Digestion That Pre-Dated Fire

The evolutionary pressure to externalize digestion predates fire by millions of years. Each pre-fire externalization strategy is a FERN register crossing in the food-processing Commons:

**1. Stone tool butchery (3.3–2.6 Mya): the oldest external Commons contribution.** The earliest known stone tools — Lomekwian flakes from Lomekwi 3, Kenya (c. 3.3 Mya, Harmand et al. 2015) and Oldowan tools from c. 2.6 Mya — were used primarily for butchering large mammal carcasses and extracting marrow from bones. Cut marks on the Lomekwi bones predate any evidence of the genus *Homo*; they were produced by *Australopithecus* or closely related hominins. Stone tools externalize the mechanical work of tearing connective tissue and crushing bone that the jaw and teeth would otherwise perform — an external digestive step that reduces the masticatory burden and opens access to high-quality protein and fat.

**2. Underground storage organs (USOs) and the tuber hypothesis (c. 2 Mya).** Wrangham et al. (1999) argue that tubers — underground storage organs (roots, rhizomes, corms, bulbs) — were the key dietary innovation that preceded and enabled cooking. USOs are extraordinarily calorie-dense, storable, and accessible year-round in the African savannah, but they require significant processing to release their starch: raw starch in tubers is largely resistant to digestion, bound in cell walls that human amylase and digestive enzymes cannot efficiently penetrate. The shift to USOs drove the evolution of more powerful tuber-processing behavior — and eventually cooking, which converts resistant starch into rapidly digestible glucose.

**3. Pounding and grinding (c. 2 Mya onward).** Stone pounding and grinding — hitting soft-tissue foods against hard surfaces, crushing seeds and nuts, mashing tubers — externalizes the mechanical stage of digestion that would otherwise occur in the mouth. This is the pre-fire Maillard reaction analog: mechanical disruption breaks cell walls, releasing starches and proteins into a more digestible form. Evidence of plant processing via pounding exists at multiple *Homo erectus* sites in East Africa.

**4. Fermentation as the first microbial Commons (deep time).** Fermentation — the microbial transformation of food through yeast, bacteria, and other microorganisms — is not a human invention. It is a biological process that operates wherever sugars, carbohydrates, and moisture exist with appropriate microbial communities. Hominids ancestral to *Homo sapiens* almost certainly encountered fermented foods naturally (overripe fruit, spontaneously fermented plant matter in cached food stores) and may have preferentially selected for them. Carrigan et al. (2015, *PNAS*) demonstrate that ADH4, the enzyme that metabolizes ethanol, underwent a functional shift approximately 10 Mya (before the hominin-ape divergence), dramatically improving ethanol metabolism capacity — suggesting that fermented fruit was a significant component of the ancestral diet before the split.

**The formal ERI identification of the pre-fire food processing chain.** Each step in the pre-fire externalization chain extends $X_{t-1}^{\text{food Commons}}$ — the accumulated processing that subsequent digestion conditions on:

- No processing: gut conditions on raw food matrix; high energetic cost of digestion; gut volume must be large
- Stone tools: gut conditions on mechanically disrupted tissue; connective tissue and bone barriers removed; fat and marrow accessible
- USO targeting: gut conditions on high-density carbohydrate stores; starch-heavy diet drives amylase gene expansion (Perry et al. 2007: AMY1 copy number increases in starch-adapted populations)
- Pounding/grinding: gut conditions on disrupted cell walls; cell-bound nutrients released
- Fermentation: gut conditions on microbially pre-processed substrates; antinutrients reduced; bioavailability increased

Each step reduces the gut's metabolic burden while expanding the nutritional range accessible from available food. The pre-fire food processing chain is the earliest documented instance of the human intellectual pattern: externalizing expensive internal processes to a Commons substrate, generating $G_{\text{coord}} > 0$ between contributors (a group of hominins sharing processed food around a carcass) through the accumulated Commons ($X_{t-1}^{\text{processed food}}: $ the butchered carcass, the pounded roots, the cached fermented tubers).

## Part II — Fire: The Thermodynamic Commons Crossing (1.8 Mya–400 kya)

### The First External Metabolism

Fire is the most consequential Commons event in hominin evolutionary history. Not because it provides warmth (though it does), not because it provides light (though it does), not because it provides protection from predators (though it does) — but because it externalizes the most expensive metabolic process in the body: the denaturation of proteins and the gelatinization of starches.

**The thermodynamic calculation.** A raw steak and a cooked steak have the same caloric content as measured by bomb calorimetry. But a human gut can extract approximately 50–80% more net energy from cooked meat than from raw meat, not because cooking adds energy but because it dramatically reduces the metabolic cost of digestion. Protein denaturation (unfolding the three-dimensional protein structure by heat) does enzymatically at body temperature what digestion otherwise has to accomplish through extended mechanical and chemical processing — and it does it before the food enters the gut. Starch gelatinization (the breakdown of crystalline starch granule structures by heat and water) converts resistant starch into rapidly fermentable glucose, accessible to pancreatic amylase and gut bacteria in ways that raw crystalline starch is not.

**The formal ERI identification: fire as the first SMELT-level external chemical Commons.**

Before fire, the gut microbiome is the only chemical processing Commons that food passes through. The gut is the internal $X_{t-1}$ — the accumulated chemical environment that every food contribution must navigate. The gut's capacity is bounded by volume and transit time: gut volume determines how much food can be processed simultaneously; transit time determines how long each food item can be chemically modified.

Fire creates an external chemical Commons that food passes through *before* entering the gut. This external Commons ($X_{t-1}^{\text{fire processing}}$) is not bounded by gut volume or transit time — it can operate at ambient scale, processing food for as long as the fire burns, at temperatures that exceed anything the gut chemistry can achieve. Fire is the first SMELT operating point that exists outside the body: the first external thermodynamic process that extends the food Commons beyond biological bounds.

The consequences for *Homo erectus* anatomy are precisely those predicted by the SMELT framework when an external Commons takes over metabolic work previously performed internally:

- **Smaller gut:** The gut shrinks because the external Commons (fire) is performing the metabolic work the gut previously had to do. *H. erectus* has a gut approximately 60% the volume of what the body-size scaling relationship (with great apes) would predict. This is the SMELT prediction: when the external Commons expands, the internal biological Commons required for the same task contracts.
- **Smaller teeth:** Cooked food is softer. The mechanical work of mastication decreases. Teeth shrink. *H. erectus* teeth are dramatically smaller than *Australopithecus* teeth — this is the dietary softening effect that PNEUMA documents as a contemporary crisis (the jaw epidemic) applied to a beneficial evolutionary adaptation.
- **Larger brain:** The energy freed from digestion (shorter gut requires less metabolic maintenance; more efficient nutrient extraction requires less gut volume; cooking reduces the metabolic cost of processing by approximately 200–400 kcal/day in the Wrangham estimate) is redirected to the most metabolically expensive organ: the brain. The human brain consumes approximately 20% of resting metabolic energy. *Australopithecus* brains are approximately 400 cc. *H. erectus* brains are approximately 900 cc. *H. sapiens* brains are approximately 1,400 cc. The trajectory is enabled by cooking.

## Part III — The Maillard Commons: The Chemistry of Cooked Flavor (400 kya–present)

### The Chemical Language That Cooking Creates

The Maillard reaction — discovered by Louis-Camille Maillard in 1912, formally characterized in the subsequent century — is the chemical process that produces the characteristic flavors, aromas, and colors of cooked food: the browning of bread crust, the sear of meat, the caramelization of onions, the roasting of coffee. It is not a single reaction but a cascade: reducing sugars react with amino acids at temperatures above approximately 140–165°C through a multi-step sequence of condensation, rearrangement (Amadori products), and fragmentation (dicarbonyl compounds, Strecker degradation), producing hundreds of flavor and aroma compounds alongside melanoidins (brown polymers) as final products.

**The Maillard reaction as a Commons Communication System.**

The formal ERI identification: the Maillard reaction is the chemistry of the cooked food Commons communicating information about nutritional state to the consumer's sensory and gut systems.

Before the Maillard reaction can occur, several conditions must be met: the food must have been heated to sufficient temperature (approximately 140°C or higher), must have contained reducing sugars and amino acids in proximity, and must have had low water activity (water vapor inhibits the reaction by competing with condensation). When these conditions are met, the Maillard reaction produces hundreds of volatile aromatic compounds that the human olfactory system detects with extremely high sensitivity. The brown color (melanoidins) and the complex aroma profile are signals that carry genuine information about the food's thermal history — and therefore about pathogen inactivation, protein denaturation state, and bioavailability.

This is $I(\text{Maillard signals};\, \text{nutritional state} \mid X_{t-1}^{\text{food Commons}}) > 0$: the Maillard reaction products carry mutual information with the food's actual nutritional and safety state. Human olfactory preference for Maillard reaction aromas is not arbitrary cultural conditioning — it is the evolutionary calibration of the sensory system to the Commons signal that fire-processed food reliably produces.

**The Maillard reaction and the gut microbiome.** Maillard reaction products (MRPs) are not only sensory signals — they also condition the gut microbiome. Some MRPs (particularly melanoidins) are partially fermentable by specific gut bacteria, acting as prebiotics that selectively stimulate beneficial microbiome members. Others (acrolein, acrylamide at high temperatures) are potential toxicants whose gut microbiome metabolism has only recently been characterized.

Venn et al. (2024, *Nutrients*): ultra-processed food MRPs — produced at industrial temperatures far exceeding those of traditional cooking — include acrolein and acrylamide that are associated with insulin resistance and oxidative stress. The formal distinction: traditional Maillard reaction products (produced at 140–180°C in the presence of water, for moderate time periods) are within the range that the human gut microbiome and liver have co-evolved to handle. Industrial Maillard products (produced at 200°C or higher, with low moisture, for extended residence times in industrial extrusion) generate chemical species that the gut Commons has no evolutionary experience with.

(2024, *Nutrients*, DOI:10.3390/nu16111674): ultra-processed foods and gut microbiome disruption. Jousse et al. ## Part IV — Fermentation: The Microbial Commons (deep time–present)

### The Oldest Cooking Technology Is Not Fire

Fermentation is older than fire. It is older than *Homo*. It is older than the genus *Australopithecus*. Fermentation — the transformation of food through the metabolic activity of microorganisms — is the original external digestion: the first time that organisms other than the consumer's own gut performed chemical processing of food before consumption.

**The evolutionary Commons of fermentation.** Carrigan et al. (2015, *PNAS*): the ADH4 mutation that dramatically improved ethanol metabolism capacity in the hominin lineage occurred approximately 10 Mya, at the divergence of the human-chimpanzee-gorilla clade from other apes. This timing corresponds to the transition from arboreal to partially terrestrial lifestyle — when fallen, fermenting fruit became a regularly encountered food source. The ADH4 functional shift is the physiological Commons adaptation to the fermentation Commons: the gut enzyme system was updated to condition on fermented food inputs before the genus *Homo* existed.

**Fermentation as the first externalized microbiome.** The gut microbiome is the internal microbial Commons that every food contribution passes through. Fermentation creates an external microbial Commons that food passes through *before* entering the gut — exactly as fire creates an external thermodynamic Commons before the gut. The two external Commons (fire and fermentation) are formally analogous but chemically orthogonal:

- Fire: external thermodynamic Commons; denatures proteins, gelatinizes starches, reduces pathogens through heat
- Fermentation: external microbial Commons; converts carbohydrates to organic acids and alcohol, generates B vitamins and amino acids, reduces antinutrients through enzymatic action, selects against pathogenic bacteria through pH reduction and competitive exclusion

When the two Commons are combined — fermented foods that are subsequently cooked, or cooked foods that are subsequently fermented — the resulting food matrix is nutritionally richer and more bioavailable than either process alone. Bread (fermented then baked), wine and beer (fermented then sometimes heat-treated), vinegar (fermented then aged), cheese (enzymatically processed and fermented), miso and soy sauce (fermented grain and legume) are the major examples.

**The human gut as a three-Commons system.** Modern understanding of human nutrition frames the gut as a three-layer processing system:

1. **External fire Commons:** Denatures proteins, gelatinizes starch, reduces pathogens
2. **External microbial Commons (fermentation):** Further processes carbohydrates, generates vitamins, establishes food microbiome
3. **Internal gut Commons (microbiome):** Ferments remaining complex carbohydrates, synthesizes vitamins, trains immune system, generates short-chain fatty acids (butyrate, propionate, acetate) that maintain gut epithelial integrity

All three Commons must be functioning for optimal nutrition. When any one is disrupted — over-processing that destroys the food microbiome; antibiotic exposure that destroys the gut microbiome; ultra-processed foods that alter the gut Commons through acrylamide and additives — the system degrades toward $G_{\text{coord}} < 0$.

**The food metagenome.** Pasolli et al. (2024, *Cell*): analysis of 2,500 food metagenomes reveals that fermented foods contain microbial communities that are distinct from gut microbiome communities but exchange members with them. The food microbiome is an external Commons that seeds and maintains the gut Commons across a human lifetime. Traditional fermented foods (yogurt, kefir, kimchi, sauerkraut, miso) introduce consistent, beneficial microbial Commons contributions to the gut. Ultra-processed foods, which are sterile (no live microbiome), pasteurized, and chemically preserved, provide no food-microbiome input — they are the fermentation equivalent of mouth breathing: cutting off a multi-million-year-old Commons channel.

**SOTA:** Carrigan et al. (2015, *PNAS*, 112:458): ADH4 functional shift and the ancient fermentation diet. Pasolli et al. (2024, *Cell*): unexplored microbial diversity from 2,500 food metagenomes and links with the human microbiome. Sonnenburg & Bäckhed (2016, *Nature*, 535:56): diet-induced alterations in gut microflora contribute to lifelong plasticity and disease risk.

## Part V — Agriculture and the Neolithic Commons Revolution (12,000–2000 BCE)

### The First Civilizational Food Commons

Agriculture — the deliberate cultivation of plant species and husbandry of animals for food — is the largest scale Commons transition in human food history before industrialization. It is also, formally, the first instance of humans constructing a multi-generational food Commons: selecting for crop and livestock traits across generations, accumulating Commons knowledge that conditions subsequent agricultural practice, and building institutions (storage, distribution, taxation, trade) around the accumulated food Commons.

**The Neolithic transition as a $G_{\text{coord}}$ event.** The Neolithic transition (c. 12,000–10,000 BCE in the Fertile Crescent; independently in China, Mesoamerica, and sub-Saharan Africa) is the food-Commons equivalent of the NOOSPHERE framework's $\rho_3 \to \rho_4$ register crossing (the Axial Age): multiple independent traditions simultaneously constructing a new Commons substrate — not because of cultural contact but because the conditions for the crossing were simultaneously met.

The crossing conditions: the end of the last Ice Age (c. 12,000 BCE) created stable, warm climates in multiple regions simultaneously. Stable climate enabled the annual plant species that would become the major cereals (wheat, barley, rice, maize, sorghum) to grow in dense, predictable stands. Dense, predictable stands of annual grasses are the pre-agricultural equivalent of the written Commons — the first food source whose accumulation could be conditioned on in advance, harvested at a known time, stored, and used as a Commons substrate for subsequent processing.

**Bread as the Maillard-fermentation synthesis.** Bread — the combination of cereal grain flour, water, yeast fermentation, and heat baking — is the most complete external digestion technology in human culinary history:

1. Grain grinding reduces particle size, exposing starch and protein to water
2. Yeast fermentation converts fermentable sugars, generates CO₂ (leavening) and organic acids (flavor, pH reduction, antinutrient reduction)
3. Bacterial fermentation (sourdough) produces additional proteolytic enzymes that partially hydrolyze gluten
4. Baking heat denatures proteins, gelatinizes starch, generates Maillard products (crust flavor, color), and sterilizes the product

The result: wheat, which in raw form contains significant antinutrients (phytates, enzyme inhibitors, WGA lectins) and difficult-to-digest starch, becomes a highly bioavailable, stable, pathogen-free, flavorful food that can be stored and traded. Bread is the food-Commons TERTIUM: fire alone (baking raw wheat paste) produces flat, dense bread; fermentation alone (unyeasted dough) produces dense, less flavorful bread; the combination produces leavened bread that neither process alone generates.

**The agricultural nutritional paradox.** Agricultural populations are, in the skeletal record, initially less healthy than the forager-horticulturalists they replaced: shorter stature, more dental caries, more nutritional deficiency diseases (iron-deficiency anemia, vitamin C deficiency, scurvy visible in skeletal bone), more infectious disease from dense living. This is the $G_{\text{coord}} < 0$ transition cost: the agricultural Commons generates more food Commons *volume* (calories per unit area) at the cost of food Commons *diversity* ($D_{\text{FERN}}$ of the diet decreases dramatically when a population shifts from hundreds of wild plant and animal species to three to five major crops).

The formal identification: agricultural food Commons concentration is the dietary equivalent of the Legalist $X_0^{\text{law}}$ — a Commons frozen at the founding moment (the set of domesticated crops and animals), maximally productive within its column space, maximally vulnerable to crop failure, pestilence, and the $\ker(F^{\text{agricultural diet}})$ of the nutritional range it cannot reach.

## Part VI — Industrialization: The Ultra-Processed Commons Catastrophe (1850–present)

### The Severing of the Three Commons

The industrial food revolution (beginning approximately 1850 CE with steam-powered milling, canning, and refrigeration; accelerating dramatically with the post-WWII development of ultra-processed food technology) represents the fastest and most disruptive change in the human food Commons in evolutionary history. Three parallel processes occur simultaneously:

**1. The destruction of the food microbiome Commons.**

Industrial processing — pasteurization, sterilization, ultra-high-temperature treatment, chemical preservation — eliminates the food microbiome that traditional fermented and fresh foods delivered to the gut. The gut microbiome, which co-evolved with the food microbiome over millions of years, is no longer being seeded with the consistent microbial Commons contributions that traditional foods provided. The gut microbiome diversity of Western populations is dramatically lower than that of traditional-diet populations in studies comparing hunter-gatherers, agricultural communities, and industrialized populations (Sonnenburg & Bäckhed 2016; Pasolli et al. 2024).

**2. The generation of novel Maillard products beyond evolutionary calibration.**

Industrial food processing uses temperatures, pressures, and moisture levels far outside the range of traditional cooking. Extrusion cooking (used to produce breakfast cereals, snack foods, pet foods) operates at 120–200°C with high shear stress and very low moisture; the Maillard reaction runs far to completion, generating acrolein, acrylamide, and advanced glycation end-products (AGEs) in concentrations that traditional cooking never produced. The gut microbiome has no evolutionary experience with these compounds; the liver's detoxification capacity is insufficient for the chronic consumption levels typical of Western diets.

**3. The jaw collapse cascade (PNEUMA connection).**

As documented in the PNEUMA framework: industrial food softening has driven the fastest craniofacial regression in the hominin fossil record. The jaw reduction that was adaptive at the rate of centuries per millimeter (cooking makes food softer → jaw reduces → brain can be larger → evolutionary advantage) is now occurring destructively at the rate of decades per millimeter (ultra-processed food makes food even softer → jaw under-develops → airway constricts → breathing compromised → sleep apnea, asthma, malocclusion epidemics). The same mechanism that enabled *H. erectus* brain expansion is, when taken to the industrial extreme, collapsing the airway of *H. sapiens*.

**The formal ERI identification: ultra-processed food as the $G_{\text{coord}} < 0$ Commons.**

Ultra-processed foods (NOVA Category 4: industrially manufactured food products with five or more ingredients, typically including additives not used in home cooking) are not merely nutritionally poor — they actively disrupt the three-Commons food processing system:

- They bypass the external fire Commons (many are not cooked by the consumer at high heat but simply heated to serving temperature)
- They destroy the external microbial Commons (sterile by design; no food microbiome contribution)
- They disrupt the internal gut Commons through Maillard toxicants, additives, and loss of dietary fiber diversity

The gut microbiome on a UPF-dominant diet is formally in $G_{\text{coord}} < 0$: the Commons has been captured by an attractor (the ultra-processed food supply) that systematically suppresses the diversity of contributions (food-microbiome species diversity, short-chain fatty acid production, immune training) that the microbiome Commons is supposed to generate. This is the Gissing *New Grub Street* pattern (GRUB STREET framework) applied to the gut: the Commons has been captured by a dominant attractor ($\alpha(\text{UPF supply}) = 1$) that selects for Commons alignment (consumption of sterile, standardized, high-palatability processed food) at the cost of Commons diversity ($D_{\text{FERN}}$ of the gut microbiome → 0).

## Seven Novel Results

**Result 1 — Cooking Is the Externalization of Digestion: The Formal Equivalence Between External Fire Commons and Internal Gut Commons Is the Central Formal Claim That Unifies 1.8 Million Years of Human Food Evolution.**

Fire extends the $X_{t-1}^{\text{food Commons}}$ by performing the most energetically expensive digestive steps — protein denaturation and starch gelatinization — before the food enters the gut. This is not a metaphor but a formal identity: the chemical reactions that heat performs externally are the same reactions that digestion performs internally, at lower efficiency and higher metabolic cost. When the external Commons (fire) performs the work, the internal Commons (gut) contracts: gut volume decreases by approximately 60% in *H. erectus* relative to what body-size scaling predicts, and this contraction frees approximately 200–400 kcal/day of metabolic energy that is redirected to brain maintenance and growth. The formal SMELT prediction — that when an external Commons expands, the internal biological Commons performing the equivalent work contracts — is confirmed by the *H. erectus* anatomical record.

**Result 2 — The ADH4 Functional Shift (10 Mya) Is the Oldest Documented Gut Adaptation to the Fermentation Commons; the Gut Microbiome-Food Microbiome Axis (Pasolli et al. 2024) Is the Modern Documentation of the Same Commons.**

The ADH4 mutation that dramatically improved ethanol metabolism in the common ancestor of humans, chimpanzees, and gorillas (Carrigan et al. 2015) is the physiological evidence that fermented food was a significant Commons contribution long before *Homo*. The 2024 *Cell* analysis of 2,500 food metagenomes (Pasolli et al.) establishes that traditional fermented foods contain microbial communities that exchange members with the gut microbiome — the external microbial Commons seeding and maintaining the internal gut Commons.
